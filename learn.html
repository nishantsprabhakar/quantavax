What this app is

A friendly map of a potential Bharat Biotech and IBM quantum partnership for vaccine discovery.

QuantaVax is a concept simulator. It shows the type of workflow a quantum-enabled vaccine discovery platform might run if Bharat Biotech’s vaccine biology, validation infrastructure, and manufacturing context were paired with IBM’s quantum computing and hybrid optimization stack.

The important point is not that software replaces scientists. It does not. The point is that better computation can help scientists spend wet-lab time on candidates that already passed a more rigorous digital screen. This is a hypothetical partnership model, not an announcement that a Bharat Biotech-IBM partnership exists.

In one sentence Design smarter, test fewer weak candidates, validate faster.

The app demonstrates decision support for early vaccine research, not clinical proof.

Structure

Folding controls whether the mRNA message is stable and readable.

mRNA is a long, flexible strand. Parts of the strand can pair with other parts, creating hairpins and loops. Those shapes affect how easily the molecule degrades, how ribosomes read it, and whether it produces the intended antigen efficiently.

The simulator’s folding arcs are a simplified visual metaphor for secondary-structure prediction, not a full atomic model.

Detailed explanations

What each scientific idea means in normal language

mRNA is a temporary instruction, not a permanent genetic change.

An mRNA vaccine provides cells with a temporary message. Cells read that message to make a selected antigen, usually a protein or part of a protein. The immune system then learns to recognize that antigen. The mRNA does not need to enter the cell nucleus, and it is naturally broken down over time.

In design terms, scientists care about the coding region, untranslated regions, chemical modifications, the cap, and the poly(A) tail. These features influence stability, translation efficiency, and immune stimulation.

Folding matters because RNA is not just a string of letters.

RNA bases can form internal pairings: A with U, G with C, and sometimes G with U. These pairings create secondary structures such as stems, loops, and hairpins. A design with the same protein output can still behave differently if its RNA structure changes.

For vaccines, folding can influence shelf stability, degradation, how ribosomes load and move, how much antigen gets produced, and whether unwanted innate immune sensing is triggered. That is why current mRNA design research treats sequence and structure as linked design problems.

Antigen confirmation asks whether the immune system can see the right target.

An antigen must be presented in a useful shape. Some immune-recognition sites are linear, meaning they come from a continuous stretch of amino acids. Others are conformational, meaning distant parts of the protein become neighbors only after the protein folds.

This is why the molecular viewer focuses on exposed hotspots and binding pockets. A promising antigen is not merely one with the right sequence; it should also expose the right surfaces in a stable, biologically plausible conformation.

Quantum computing is used here as a search aid, not a miracle engine.

Many folding problems can be framed as optimization problems: out of many possible pairings and shapes, which arrangement has the best energy and constraint score? Some RNA structure formulations can be mapped into QUBO or Ising-style models, which are formats quantum algorithms can work with.

Recent IBM and Moderna work applied CVaR-based variational quantum algorithms to mRNA secondary-structure prediction at sequence lengths up to 60 nucleotides and up to 80 qubits. That is meaningful research progress, but it is still early compared with full production-scale vaccine design.

What the algorithms mean

without jargon overload
QUBO

Turn biology into choices

The app treats possible base pairings as yes-or-no decisions. The scoring model rewards stable pairings and penalizes impossible or conflicting ones.

CVaR

Keep the best tail

Instead of trusting the average result, CVaR focuses on the best low-energy group of sampled answers. That is useful when the search produces many noisy possibilities.

VQE/VQA

Let classical and quantum work together

A classical computer updates parameters; quantum hardware samples hard combinations. The loop repeats until the score improves.

Bayesian triage

Update confidence as evidence arrives

Stability, epitope exposure, binding signal, manufacturability, and lab capacity are combined into a practical decision score.

How each algorithm is working

step by step
Algorithm 01

QUBO-CVaR mRNA fold search

This is the part that asks: “Given this mRNA sequence, which internal base pairings create a stable and biologically plausible fold?”

1. Convert biology into binary choices

Every possible base pair is treated like a yes-or-no switch. For example, position 8 pairing with position 42 is one possible decision. The model allows valid RNA pairings such as A:U and G:C, and it can include weaker wobble pairings such as G:U.

2. Build the QUBO score

QUBO means Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization. In plain language, it is a scoring table for yes-or-no decisions. Good pairings reduce the score. Conflicting choices, such as one base trying to pair with two different bases, add penalties.

3. Sample many candidate folds

A quantum-compatible optimizer searches combinations of switches. The simulator visualizes this as candidate states moving through the quantum algorithm panel. In real research, this can be run as a hybrid workflow using classical optimization wrapped around quantum sampling.

4. Apply CVaR filtering

CVaR means Conditional Value at Risk. Instead of trusting the average of all samples, it focuses on the best low-energy tail. For folding, that means the algorithm pays more attention to the most promising structures and less attention to noisy weak samples.

5. Return fold candidates

The output is a ranked set of possible mRNA folds. In the app, these appear as arcs in the folding energy landscape. Lower-energy, non-conflicting pairings are treated as more promising for stability and translation analysis.

Algorithm 02

VQE/VQA conformer confirmation

This is the part that asks: “Does the antigen likely settle into a shape that exposes useful immune-recognition regions?”

1. Start with candidate conformations

A conformer is a possible 3D arrangement of a molecule. Classical structure tools can generate plausible antigen shapes and docking priors. QuantaVax treats those as starting guesses rather than final answers.

2. Encode energy as an objective

The algorithm needs a score that says whether one shape is more plausible than another. Lower-energy structures are usually more physically plausible, although biology also depends on solvent, temperature, formulation, and experimental context.

3. Run a variational loop

VQE, or Variational Quantum Eigensolver, uses a parameterized quantum circuit. The quantum side samples energy behavior. The classical side adjusts the circuit parameters. The loop repeats until the sampled score improves.

4. Compare exposed hotspots

Once promising shapes are found, the model checks whether important surfaces are exposed. In the app, colored hotspots represent predicted epitope exposure or binding confidence. These are decision-support signals, not experimental proof.

5. Output confirmation confidence

The final confidence score combines conformer plausibility, predicted exposure, binding signal, and noise control. It helps decide whether the candidate deserves wet-lab validation.

Algorithm 03

Bayesian wet-lab triage

This is the part that asks: “Given all the evidence so far, which candidates should Bharat Biotech test first?”

1. Start with a prior

A prior is the starting belief about a candidate before new evidence is added. A candidate may begin with moderate confidence simply because it matches the target design brief.

2. Add structure evidence

If the mRNA fold looks stable and the antigen shape looks plausible, confidence rises. If folding is unstable, noisy, or conflicts with translation goals, confidence drops.

3. Add biological and operational evidence

The model then considers epitope exposure, binding signal, manufacturability, and wet-lab capacity. This matters because a beautiful computational structure is not useful if it cannot be made, tested, or advanced realistically.

4. Update the posterior

The posterior is the revised confidence after evidence is included. In the simulator, this becomes the confirmation confidence, candidate count, and pathway compression readouts.

5. Produce the shortlist

The output is a smaller set of candidates for validation. This is where time and cost savings may appear: fewer weak candidates consume expensive lab slots.

Questions people naturally ask

tap to expand
Does this app prove a vaccine will work?

No. It is a simulation and product concept. Real vaccine proof requires wet-lab validation, animal studies where appropriate, clinical trials, manufacturing quality control, regulatory review, and safety monitoring.

Why not just use AI instead of quantum computing?

AI is already important in biology, and a real platform would likely use AI, classical physics, and quantum methods together. Quantum algorithms are interesting for specific optimization and sampling problems where the number of possible states grows very quickly. They are not a general replacement for AI.

Why does the simulator show savings?

Early research wastes money when too many weak candidates reach expensive validation. The savings model assumes better computational triage reduces the number of poor candidates sent to wet-lab work and shortens the time spent searching structure space. These are scenario estimates, not guaranteed business outcomes.

Why are the numbers adjustable?

Discovery cost and timeline vary by pathogen, platform maturity, lab capacity, data quality, regulatory path, and validation requirements. A responsible simulator should expose its assumptions instead of pretending one universal number is accurate for every vaccine program.
